# Self-Assessment

Version: 0.1.0
Last updated: 2026-06-04

## How To Use This

Assess one scope at a time:

- one team
- one function
- one recurring workflow
- one customer-facing process
- one internal operating process

Avoid scoring the whole company unless the company is very small. Mixed maturity is normal.

## Assessment Steps

1. Define the scope in one sentence.
2. List current AI tools and where they are used.
3. Collect evidence from recent work, not intentions.
4. Score the four dimensions from 1 to 5.
5. Score the two cross-cutting spines from 1 to 5.
6. Create a transformation profile if you need to explain the broader change.
7. Identify the binding constraint.
8. Choose the next capability move.

## Dimension Scoring Matrix

| Dimension | 1 - Exploring | 2 - Assisted | 3 - Embedded | 4 - Orchestrated | 5 - AI-native |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Direction & Use Cases | AI ideas are scattered or opportunistic. | A few useful use cases are known, but prioritisation is informal. | Priority AI use cases are selected by value, risk, and feasibility. | AI use cases are managed as a small portfolio with owners and measures. | AI investment choices are part of normal business strategy and operating review. |
| Workflow & Adoption | Individual experiments. No standard workflow. | AI used for recurring personal tasks. | AI included in documented team workflows. | Multi-step AI workflows run across tools or systems. | Selected functions are designed AI-first by default. |
| Data & Context | AI uses generic prompts and public knowledge. | Reusable prompts or examples exist. | Curated context, examples, and source material are provided for key workflows. | AI workflows use approved knowledge sources or connected systems. | Context quality is actively managed as part of operations. |
| Tools & Integration | Personal or ad hoc tools. | Common approved tools are available. | Tools are embedded into the workflow where work happens. | Tools, automations, or agents are connected across steps. | Production-grade AI tooling includes monitoring, evaluation, and improvement loops. |

## Spine Scoring Matrix

| Spine | 1 - Exploring | 2 - Assisted | 3 - Embedded | 4 - Orchestrated | 5 - AI-native |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| People & Accountability | People are unsure what AI is good for. No clear owner exists. | People can prompt and review basic outputs. Managers expect human review. | People decompose tasks, verify results, and own workflow outcomes. | People design oversight, escalation, and handoffs for AI-assisted workflows. | People govern, improve, and teach AI-native operating patterns. |
| Governance & Evidence | No clear rules, review expectations, or value evidence. | Basic acceptable-use rules and human review expectations. | Workflow-level review gates, quality checks, and basic value measures. | Audit trails, escalation rules, cost controls, and risk-based approvals. | Portfolio-level governance, performance metrics, and continuous improvement. |

## Interpreting The Result

Use this rule:

1. The dimension level is the highest level where at least three of four dimensions meet that level and no dimension is more than one level behind.
2. The spine cap is the lower score of the two cross-cutting spines.
3. The overall working level is the lower of the dimension level and the spine cap.
4. The binding constraint is the lowest-scoring material dimension or spine that blocks the target workflow.
5. The next move should raise the binding constraint, not polish the strongest area.

Example:

- Dimension scores: Direction 3, Workflow 3, Data 2, Tools 3.
- Spine scores: People & Accountability 2, Governance & Evidence 2.
- Dimension level: 3.
- Spine cap: 2.
- Working level: 2.
- Binding constraint: Data & Context, People & Accountability, and Governance & Evidence.
- Better next move: add context packs, named ownership, review gates, and team training for one workflow.
- Worse next move: buy more tools.

## Evidence Checklist

Use observable evidence such as:

- examples of recent AI-assisted work
- workflow documentation
- shared prompts or context packs
- review checklists
- usage patterns
- before-and-after cycle time or quality measures
- error logs or escalation examples
- customer, staff, or manager feedback

Do not use:

- self-reported confidence alone
- tool licenses purchased
- one impressive demo
- vendor promises
- a roadmap that has not changed work yet

## Confidence Rating

| Confidence | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| High | Multiple recent examples support the score. |
| Medium | Some evidence exists, but the pattern is not yet stable. |
| Low | Score is mostly inferred. More evidence is needed before acting on it. |

## Optional Transformation Profile

Use this when the assessment is for leadership planning, not just a workflow improvement.

| Capability | Score | Evidence Used | What It Means |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| AI Working Skill |  |  | Can people use AI effectively in real work? |
| Work Design Depth |  |  | Are people redesigning work at the right level? |
| Reliable AI Operations |  |  | Can AI-assisted work run safely and reliably? |
| Organizational Adaptability |  |  | Can the organization adapt as technology changes? |

The transformation profile should explain the next investment. If the maturity score is Level 3 but Reliable AI Operations is Level 2, the next move is not more automation. It is quality, measurement, review, and risk control.
