# SMB AI Maturity Model

Version: 0.1.0
Last updated: 2026-06-04

A practical starter model for small and midsize businesses assessing how AI is actually used in their work, and what has to change for AI to become a real operating capability.

The maturity model is intentionally simple. It helps a team identify its current level, the constraint blocking the next level, and the smallest useful capability move. The transformation model adds a second lens: whether the next investment is AI working skill, work design depth, reliable AI operations, or organizational adaptability.

This is not a certification scheme, a vendor scorecard, or a substitute for security, legal, or compliance review.

## What This Is For

Use this when you need to:

- assess AI adoption without turning it into a large consulting exercise
- compare teams or workflows using the same language
- identify whether the blocker is workflow design, people capability, data context, tools, or governance
- explain what kind of transformation is needed beyond the maturity score
- choose the next step without overbuilding
- brief leaders on what "more mature" actually means

## Maturity Model At A Glance

| Level | Name | What It Means |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Exploring | Individuals experiment with AI, but usage is ad hoc and not measured. |
| 2 | Assisted | Common tools are available, used routinely, and reviewed by humans. |
| 3 | Embedded | Priority workflows include AI as a documented, repeatable step. |
| 4 | Orchestrated | Connected AI workflows or agents run multi-step processes with approval gates. |
| 5 | AI-native | Selected functions are designed AI-first, with governance, measurement, and continuous improvement. |

## Transformation Model At A Glance

The maturity model answers: "Where are we?"

The transformation model answers: "What needs to change next?"

| Capability | What It Looks At | Typical Next Move |
| --- | --- | --- |
| AI Working Skill | Whether people can use AI effectively in real work. | Build role-specific practice, task decomposition, output review, and shared examples. |
| Work Design Depth | Whether the work itself is being redesigned, not just prompted faster. | Redesign workflows around outcomes, trade-offs, handoffs, and decision points. |
| Reliable AI Operations | Whether AI-assisted work can run safely and consistently. | Add review gates, evidence, measurement, safety boundaries, and escalation paths. |
| Organizational Adaptability | Whether the organization can adjust as AI capability changes. | Add a cadence for updating roles, workflows, context, governance, and investment choices. |

Use this lens after the maturity assessment. A team can have a reasonable maturity score and still be constrained by one transformation capability. For example, strong individual AI skill with weak reliable operations means the next move is not more automation; it is quality, measurement, and risk control.

## Core Assessment Rule

Do not average your way into maturity.

AI maturity is constrained by the weakest material dimension or spine. A team with advanced tools but weak governance is not operating at an advanced maturity level. A team with strong strategy but no repeatable workflow is not mature either.

Use the average score only as a trend indicator. Use the lowest material dimension or spine as the binding constraint.

## Public Boundary

The example company, workflows, and scores are fictional. The model avoids private organization names, private URLs, internal rollout targets, customer data, local machine paths, and time-sensitive vendor pricing claims.
